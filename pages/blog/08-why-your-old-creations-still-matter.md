Generative systems move fast. Models improve, defaults change, and whole styles go from impossible to trivial in months.

That velocity is exciting — but it creates a quiet risk:

> Your older creations stop making sense, both aesthetically and technically.

We care a lot about avoiding that outcome. This post explains why, and what we do about it.

## 1. Creations are more than snapshots

On the surface, a creation is just:

- An image or video  
- Some text around it  
- A timestamp

But to the person who made it, it often encodes:

- A **moment in time** — what they were exploring, learning, or feeling  
- A specific **model behavior** that may not exist anymore  
- A **workflow** (prompts, tweaks, retries) they could build on later

If we treat creations as disposable artifacts from an obsolete engine, we:

- Erase that history  
- Make it harder for people to see their own progress  
- Discourage deep investment in the platform

We’d rather treat them as a living archive.

## 2. Model upgrades without erasing the past

When we ship model updates, we try to avoid the feeling of:

- “Everything I liked before is now wrong.”

Some of the strategies:

- **Stable presets**: we maintain presets whose behavior we test across model versions, so they don’t drift wildly.  
- **Pinned configurations**: certain important creations and workflows are tied to explicit model/config versions under the hood.  
- **Soft migrations**: where we do change defaults, we try to move gradually and explain why.

We can’t freeze everything forever — but we can avoid whiplash.

## 3. Remixing old work on new rails

One of the most rewarding behaviors we see is:

- Someone revisits an older creation  
- Tries the same idea with new tools  
- Sees what they’ve learned in the gap between

To support that, we aim for:

- **Readable history**: you can see enough about what went into an older piece to meaningfully remix it.  
- **Safe “upgrade” paths**: where we offer ways to re‑run or upscale old work, we make it clear what will and won’t stay the same.  
- **Non‑destructive flows**: new attempts don’t overwrite the original; they live alongside it.

That way, “redo with the new model” feels like a creative choice, not a forced migration.

## 4. When we have to break things

Sometimes, we *do* have to make changes that break perfect reproducibility:

- Safety improvements  
- Infrastructure shifts  
- Removing behavior that was never intended in the first place

In those cases we try to:

- Be **explicit**: acknowledge that something changed rather than pretending it didn’t.  
- Offer **alternatives**: explain what you can do now that’s closest to the old behavior.  
- Preserve **artifacts**: keep the original media and as much context as we safely can.

We’d rather take a difficult, well‑explained step than silently let the system drift under your feet.

## 5. Why this matters for the future

Parascene is still early. If we’re lucky, people will be using it — or whatever it becomes — for a long time.

If we get this right:

- Your earliest experiments will still **feel like part of your story**, not an embarrassing artifact of “old Parascene.”  
- We can keep shipping new capabilities **without demanding amnesia** every few months.  
- The platform starts to look less like a novelty and more like a real creative home.

That’s the bar we measure ourselves against when we’re deciding whether a change is worth it.
