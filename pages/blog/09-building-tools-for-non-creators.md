title: "Building Tools for People Who Don’t Call Themselves Creators (Yet)"

---

If you ask our early users how they describe themselves, you’ll hear:

- Artists  
- Designers  
- Engineers  
- Creative‑adjacent job titles

But a growing chunk of Parascene’s users don’t think of themselves as “creators” at all.

This post is about what we’ve learned from that group, and how it feeds back into the product.

## 1. Different goals, different friction

People who don’t identify as creators often:

- Want **an outcome**, not a practice — a banner, a gift, a moodboard.  
- Have **less tolerance for failure loops** — three bad results in a row feel like confirmation they’re “not good at this.”  
- Worry more about **looking foolish** than about pushing the medium forward.

Designing for them means:

- Shorter paths from “idea” to “something I can actually use.”  
- Gentler defaults that make *reasonable* choices on their behalf.  
- Clearer framing around what’s normal to struggle with.

## 2. Onboarding without homework

We try to avoid experiences that feel like:

> “Welcome to Parascene! Please read this 20‑page prompt‑engineering guide.”

Instead, we aim for:

- A **single obvious path** for a first creation.  
- Examples that are **relatable** (birthday card, playlist cover, cozy background) rather than pure demos of model power.  
- Copy that acknowledges uncertainty: it’s okay not to know the “right” words yet.

Once someone has a small win — something they like enough to keep — they’re dramatically more willing to explore deeper controls.

## 3. Guardrails that feel like guidance, not scolding

Non‑creators are often the first to fall into:

- Vague prompts (“cool art please”)  
- Overly literal expectations (“I asked for X, why isn’t it exactly X?”)

We can’t fix that with warnings alone.

We try to build:

- **Inline prompt suggestions** that steer toward concrete language.  
- **Before/after examples** that show how a small tweak can change results.  
- UI that **rewards iteration**, not just first‑try perfection (e.g. easy “Try again” and “Make it weirder” actions).

The goal is to make the tool feel like a collaborator, not a pass/fail test.

## 4. Space to just enjoy the feed

Not everyone will become an active creator, and that’s okay.

Some people:

- Mainly want to **browse and react**.  
- Occasionally want to **save or share** something that resonates.  
- Only create when there’s a specific, external reason.

We design for that by:

- Making the feed a **first‑class experience**, not just a funnel into the create page.  
- Letting people **follow, favorite, and comment** without ever feeling obligated to post.  
- Making it easy to **graduate** into creating when they’re ready, without moving to a different part of the app.

If we do this right, the line between “audience” and “creator” becomes much softer.

## 5. Why we keep the word “creator” anyway

We still use “creator” a lot internally and in the product.

Not because we expect everyone to adopt that identity, but because:

- It’s a reminder that **every user has the option** to make things, not just consume.  
- It shapes our bias toward **agency** — you’re not just a metric in someone else’s funnel.  
- It keeps us honest about **who we’re building for** when we make tradeoffs.

If you don’t think of yourself as a creator yet, that’s fine. Parascene should still feel like a good place to be.

But if something here eventually nudges you to try making something of your own, that’s a win we care a lot about.
