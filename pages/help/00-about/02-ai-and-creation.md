## How do we see AI and art?

AI models are tools. They can be useful, surprising, and sometimes clumsy — but they don't get to decide what is or isn’t “art,” and neither do we. We’re not interested in weaponizing that word or using it to draw a hard line between “real” and “fake” creators. What matters here is that people have ideas, express them, and are reasonably honest about how they made the work.

We also think “art” ultimately stands on its own in the mind of the person who encounters it. However something was made, the experience belongs to the viewer: what they notice, what they feel, what it changes for them. Artists who refuse to acknowledge that basic relationship—who see only process and never audience—are, in our view, missing something essential about what it means to make work for other humans.

## Is parascene an AI‑first platform?

No. parascene is not “about AI” first. We welcome creations that use models heavily, creations that use them a little, and creations that don’t touch AI at all. If someone wants to sketch, photograph, write code, or mix all of the above with a model, that’s their call. Our job is to give them room to experiment and an audience to meet them.

Our view of “AI art” also grows out of a longer‑standing respect for generative work in general. There is a long history of artists using rules, chance, and machines—automatic drawing, algorithmic music, plotters, systems art—to make something worth looking at. We see modern model‑driven work as part of that family: a mix of intention, process, and surprise. Randomness, accidents, and emergent detail are not bugs to us; they’re part of what makes a contemporary artistic experience feel alive.

## Why do we defend access and generative tools?

We care about access. Saying “no” to AI across the board often ends up saying “no” to people who don’t have the money, time, or training to hire or become traditional illustrators. We think there’s real value in lowering that barrier so more ideas can move around, not fewer, while still respecting working artists and the realities of markets and attention.

There is also a wide range of ways to use these tools. Asking a model to clean up the lines on your own sketch, or to nudge a photo toward a look you already designed, is still overwhelmingly human work. When critics treat this as theft while accepting every other digital shortcut—or insist that nothing is “real” unless the artist also made the paper, paint, and camera—that doesn’t feel very honest. Lumping those uses in with plagiarism and “AI doom” talking points mostly muddies the water instead of helping anyone.

## Who should hold the keys to the models?

We also care about who holds the keys. A core part of parascene is that users can run models on their own systems and contribute those servers to the network instead of being locked to a single vendor. We do integrate frontier models from large providers, but our long‑term bet is on a rich ecosystem of user‑run and community‑run models that individuals and small communities can understand, tune, and, when they want to, walk away from.

Ironically, the loudest, bleakest takes about AI often help the biggest corporate providers the most. Moral panic and fatalism push people toward a handful of central platforms instead of toward tools those same people can understand and even run themselves. We’d rather put that energy into building alternatives—places where individuals and small communities actually have leverage over how models are used.

## How do we want people to use AI here?

If you do use AI as an artist here, we’d rather see it deepen your craft than replace it. Treat the model as a collaborator and a mirror. Keep learning to draw, write, photograph, design—whatever your lane is. Our hope is that parascene helps people become more creative over time, not less, regardless of how much machinery is involved along the way.

And if your goal isn’t to be “an artist” at all, that’s fine too. If you just want to view or make cool pictures, weird videos, or things that make your friends laugh, you don’t need to carry all this art theory on your back. Have fun, experiment, and share what you like—the rest can stay background noise.
